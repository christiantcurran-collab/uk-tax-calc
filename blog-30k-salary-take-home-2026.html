How Much Will You Take Home on a £30,000 Salary in 2026/27?

A £30,000 salary is close to the UK median income and a common benchmark for graduates a few years into their career. But after income tax and National Insurance, how much actually lands in your bank account? We’ve run the numbers for 2026/27.

Your monthly take-home pay

On a £30,000 annual salary with no student loan and the standard tax code (1257L), here's what you'll take home:

Gross salary £30,000 £2,500/month
Personal allowance £12,570 Tax-free
Income tax (20%) −£3,486 −£290.50/month
National Insurance (8%) −£1,394 −£116.21/month
Take-home pay £25,120 £2,093/month

That's an effective tax rate of 16.3%. For every £1 you earn, you keep about 84p.

How does this compare to nearby salaries?

Wondering whether a pay rise makes a real difference? Here's how salaries around the £30k mark compare after tax:

  • £27,500: Take home £1,917/month
  • £28,000: Take home £1,946/month
  • £30,000: Take home £2,093/month
  • £32,000: Take home £2,213/month
  • £35,000: Take home £2,393/month

A £5,000 raise from £30k to £35k would put an extra £300 per month in your pocket — not the full £417 because of the 28% combined deduction rate (20% income tax + 8% NI).

Can you afford a mortgage on £30k?

Lenders typically offer 4 to 4.5 times your salary. On £30,000, that means a potential mortgage of £120,000 – £135,000.

With a 10% deposit on a £150,000 property, your mortgage of £135,000 would cost roughly £760/month at a 4.5% interest rate over 25 years. That's 36% of your gross income — right at the upper limit of what most lenders will accept.

Tips to boost your take-home pay

  • Salary sacrifice pension: Contributing via salary sacrifice reduces your NI as well as tax, saving you more than a personal contribution
  • Marriage allowance: If your partner earns under £12,570, they can transfer £1,260 of their allowance to you — saving £252/year
  • Check your tax code: An incorrect tax code could mean you're overpaying — check your payslip or personal tax account
  • Claim working from home relief: If your employer requires you to work from home, you may be able to claim £6/week tax-free
